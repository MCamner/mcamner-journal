# Le Samouraï

*Film 008 · Dir. Jean-Pierre Melville · 1967*

Rating: essential

---

The film opens on a room. Grey walls, a single bed, a coat on a hook, a birdcage in the corner. Jef Costello lies still on the bed in his hat and trench coat, as if he arrived and never unpacked, as if the room is temporary and always has been. The bird watches.

Melville gives Jef almost no dialogue and no backstory. What we get instead is ritual. He checks the stolen car with a practiced sequence of moves. He adjusts his hat brim to a precise angle in the mirror. He establishes alibi witnesses with the careful distribution of a man who plans not because he is anxious but because planning is the only thing he trusts. The ritual is not preparation. It is identity.

Alain Delon plays Jef as someone who has removed everything unnecessary from himself — emotion, spontaneity, attachment. What remains is competence and a code. The code does not have much content. It mostly says: do the job, do not talk, do not get caught. Within that constraint he operates with complete precision.

Le Samouraï is a film about economy. Every scene contains exactly what it needs and nothing else. Melville's direction is the same — no coverage shots, no explanation, no emotional signaling. The audience is trusted to follow what is happening without being guided through it.

The Paris of the film is almost entirely grey. The streets, the metro corridors, the police headquarters — all the same cool tone. Only the jazz club where the pianist watches Jef has color. That detail is not decorative.

What the film understands is that a code without flexibility is eventually self-defeating. The code keeps Jef alive and competent and also makes every situation he encounters slightly unresolvable. There is no ending available to him that works within the code. Melville does not dramatize this — he just shows it.

The last shot is the result of a man who understood what the film was about before the audience did.
