# A Pattern Language

*Book 002 · Christopher Alexander · 1977*

Rating: essential

---

253 patterns. Each one names something that works. Not rules — observations. The book describes the built world at the level of felt experience, not architectural abstraction.

The patterns nest: cities contain neighborhoods, neighborhoods contain buildings, buildings contain rooms. Each scale has its own logic. Alexander's claim is that the patterns are not invented — they are discovered. They exist wherever people feel at home.

The section on light is the one that stays. Every room should have light on two sides of it. A room lit from one direction produces glare and flat shadow. Two directions produces depth. The eye relaxes. It seems obvious once stated. It isn't obvious until then.

What A Pattern Language understands is that design decisions are not isolated. A window choice affects the room, which affects the house, which affects the street. The book gives you language to see those connections. Once you have the language, you can't stop using it.

The patterns are not instructions. Alexander is explicit about this. You adapt them to context. The point is not to replicate what worked somewhere else — it is to understand why it worked and apply that understanding to your situation.

This is also a book about software, about cities, about organisations. Anywhere you need to ask: what are the underlying patterns that make this work? Alexander's method applies. The domain changes. The thinking doesn't.
