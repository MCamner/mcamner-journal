>> victorinox farmer

The choice of model matters. Not the Officer's knife, not the Spartan, not the SwissChamp with its seventeen tools arranged like a catalogue of hypothetical problems. The Farmer. Eight tools selected for actual use rather than completeness.

The saw is what distinguishes it. A real wood saw, not a decorative serration. Combined with the blade, the reamer, the can opener, and the wire stripper, the Farmer describes a model of the world where problems are physical and the answer is usually cutting, piercing, or prying something. That model is more often correct than it appears from a desk.

The red cellidor scales are not a choice — they are Victorinox's identity, carried since the original Swiss Army issue. They have the quality of things that stopped needing to justify themselves decades ago and simply became what they are. In a pocket alongside keys and coins, the red is a marker, not a style decision.

A tool with a long memory means one that has solved enough specific problems across enough years that it has accumulated a kind of history. This one has been used on wood, wire, packaging, rope, and food — in that order, approximately, on a single afternoon once. The Farmer makes that afternoon possible without requiring you to prepare for it. That is the only metric that matters.
